Welcome & Core Philosophy

⚔️

Jaxon, welcome. You are about to take on the most creative, rewarding, and occasionally chaotic role in tabletop gaming — the Dungeon Master. You are the author, director, narrator, and referee all at once. Your players are the heroes. Your job is to make them feel like heroes.

The One Rule That Rules Them All

The goal is not to "win." The goal is for everyone at the table — including you — to have a great time. That's it. Everything else is just tools to get there.

What the DM Actually Does

  • Narrates the world — describes locations, smells, sounds, and atmosphere
  • Plays every NPC — from the nervous innkeeper to the ancient dragon
  • Adjudicates rules — decides what happens when the rulebook is unclear
  • Creates challenges — puzzles, combat, social encounters, moral dilemmas
  • Reacts to players — their choices drive the story, your job is to respond

The Three Pillars of D&D

⚔️ Combat

Turn-based fights with dice, tactics, and high stakes. The most rule-heavy pillar — but satisfying when it clicks.

🗺️ Exploration

Discovering the world — dungeons, wilderness, cities. Players ask "what's here?" You describe it.

🗣️ Social Interaction

Talking to NPCs, persuading guards, negotiating with merchants. Often the most memorable moments.

🎭 Your Balance

Lean into whichever your group enjoys most. Watch their energy — when they lean forward, give them more of that.

The DM's Mindset

  • "Yes, and…" — Build on what players do, don't block them
  • Imperfect is perfect — No session goes as planned. That's normal. Roll with it.
  • Rulings, not rules — When unsure of a rule, make a fair call and look it up later
  • The players are co-authors — The best D&D moments come from players surprising you
  • You cannot fail — As long as people are engaged, you're doing it right
First-Time DM Encouragement

Every legendary DM had a shaky first session. Matt Mercer had nervous moments. Chris Perkins made mistakes. Your players are your friends — they want you to succeed. Lower the bar for yourself: if everyone laughs and nobody dies bored, you nailed it.

02

Pre-Session Preparation

📜

Good preparation doesn't mean scripting every word — it means giving yourself enough structure to improvise confidently. Prepare the scaffolding; let the players fill in the story.

The 2-Hour Rule

For a first session, 2 hours of prep is plenty. More than that and you risk over-scripting. Your players will go off-script within 10 minutes anyway.

Step 1 — Define Your Hook

Your session needs a clear opening situation. Something happens that pulls the players into action. Make it immediate. Make it visible.

  • A village is attacked by bandits and they're the only people who can help
  • They wake up in a dungeon with no memory of how they got there
  • A merchant hires them to escort a mysterious crate across the mountains
  • A local mine has gone silent — workers missing, foreman hasn't returned

Don't start with a 20-minute backstory monologue. Start in the action.

Step 2 — Plan Your Locations

Prep 2–3 locations, no more for a first session. Each location needs:

Location Template
Name & Typee.g., "The Amber Flagon" (tavern)
3 Sensory DetailsSmell, sound, one visual
Who's Here1–2 named NPCs
What Can HappenCombat? Info? Purchase?
Exit OptionsWhere can players go next?

Step 3 — Create 2–3 NPCs

You don't need a cast of thousands. Prepare 2–3 NPCs that players will definitely meet, and improvise the rest as needed.

For each NPC, know:

  • Name — Keep it pronounceable. "Brom" is fine. "Xzytheriax" is a mouthful.
  • One physical detail — A scar, a nervous habit, a distinctive voice
  • Their goal — What do they want right now?
  • Their secret — Something they're hiding, even if it never comes up
  • Their attitude toward the players — Friendly? Suspicious? Desperate?

Step 4 — Plan One Encounter

Prepare at least one combat encounter. Use this formula for balanced first sessions:

Easy Encounter Formula (4 Players, Level 1)

3–4 Goblins or 2 Wolves or 1 Bandit Captain + 2 Bandits. Anything in the Monster Manual marked "CR ¼" or lower is appropriate. When in doubt, use fewer enemies — you can always add reinforcements mid-combat.

Step 5 — Prepare Your Notes Format

Keep your session notes on index cards or a single page. Avoid scrolling through long documents mid-game. Recommended layout:

  • Card 1: Hook sentence + opening scene description
  • Card 2: Location A — sensory details + NPCs
  • Card 3: Combat encounter — enemy stat blocks
  • Card 4: Possible endings / "what happens if they fail"
Avoid These Prep Mistakes
  • Don't write dialogue word-for-word — you'll freeze when players respond unexpectedly
  • Don't prep a railroad — give players at least 2 ways to approach every situation
  • Don't prep more than you can use in 3–4 hours — unused prep isn't wasted, save it for next session
03

Running Your First Session

🎲

The Session Structure

A standard 3–4 hour session follows a loose arc. Don't be rigid about it, but having this in your head helps you pace:

PhaseDurationWhat Happens
Opening15–20 minSet the scene. Establish where players are, what they see. Hook them into the situation.
Rising Action60–90 minExploration, social interaction, investigation. Players gather info and make decisions.
Climax45–60 minThe main combat or dramatic confrontation. High stakes, real consequences.
Resolution15–20 minWrap up, reward loot/XP, tease what's coming next. End on a hook.

Opening a Session (Word-for-Word Starter)

If you're not sure how to open, steal this formula and fill in the blanks:

Opening Script Template

"The year is [year/era]. You find yourselves in [location]. It is [time of day], and [sensory detail 1 — smell/sound]. [Describe what they see]. You've come here because [brief reason they're together]. As you [current activity], [the inciting event happens]. What do you do?"

End every scene setup with "What do you do?" — it passes the spotlight to the players and signals it's their turn.

Pacing — Reading the Room

  • Players looking bored? Introduce a new element — an NPC rushes in, something explodes, a stranger drops a note at their table
  • Players arguing over a plan? Let them. This is good roleplay. After 5 minutes, apply light pressure — "you hear footsteps approaching"
  • Players overwhelmed? Have an NPC give a gentle nudge in the right direction — an ally, a note, a sudden obvious clue
  • Players flying through content? Skip to the next interesting thing. Never pad with filler.

Handling Unexpected Situations

Players will do something you didn't plan for. This is a feature, not a bug. Tools for handling it:

  • "That's interesting — tell me more about what you're trying to do." Buys you thinking time.
  • Assign a DC and roll. Almost any action can be resolved with an ability check.
  • Say yes. If their idea is creative and doesn't break the game, let it work. They'll love you for it.
  • Delay consequences. "The door slides open... you'll find out what's inside when you step through." Use tension to stall while you improvise.
  • Take a 5-minute break. Totally legitimate. "Let's grab drinks — I need a moment." Nobody minds.

Ending Your Session

Always end on something memorable. Options:

  • A mystery revealed that opens new questions
  • A villain escapes and vows revenge
  • A reward received + a new hook hinted at
  • A quiet moment of roleplay between characters

After the session, do a 5-minute "debrief" — ask players what they loved and what confused them. This feedback is gold.

Combat Guide

🗡️

How Combat Works — Step by Step

  1. Surprise check — If one side ambushes the other, they get a surprise round
  2. Roll Initiative — Everyone rolls d20 + Dexterity modifier. Go from highest to lowest.
  3. Take turns — Each creature gets a turn. On a turn: Move + Action + Bonus Action
  4. Repeat — Rounds continue until combat ends (all enemies down, flee, surrender)

Action Economy — What Can You Do on Your Turn

Action (1 per turn)

  • Attack
  • Cast a spell
  • Dash (double movement)
  • Disengage (no opportunity attacks)
  • Help (give ally advantage)
  • Hide
  • Use an item

Bonus Action & Reaction

Bonus Action: Only if a feature or spell grants it (off-hand attack, certain spells)


Reaction: Once per round, triggered by an event (Opportunity Attack, Shield spell, Counterspell)

Making an Attack — The Flow

  1. Announce target and attack type
  2. Roll d20 + Attack Modifier
  3. Compare to target's Armor Class (AC)
  4. If equal or higher — hit! Roll damage dice
  5. If lower — miss. Describe the near-miss dramatically
  6. On a 20 — Critical Hit! Roll damage dice twice
  7. On a 1 — Critical Miss. Something goes wrong (weapon fumble, etc.)

Difficulty Balancing for First Sessions

Common New DM Mistake

New DMs often make encounters too hard, killing characters in session one. Or too easy, making combat feel pointless. Start easy — you can always add a second wave of enemies if the fight is going too smoothly.

DifficultyFor 4 Level-1 PlayersFeel
Easy3 Goblins (CR ¼)Satisfying win, minimal resource drain
Medium4 Goblins + 1 Goblin BossTense but manageable
Hard1 Ogre (CR 2)Someone probably goes down

When a Player Drops to 0 HP

  1. Player is unconscious and falls prone
  2. Each turn they roll a Death Save (d20): 10+ = success, 9 or lower = failure
  3. 3 successes = stabilized (still unconscious but not dying)
  4. 3 failures = dead
  5. Any ally can use their Action to stabilize them with a DC 10 Medicine check (or a Healer's Kit)
  6. Any healing immediately brings them back up with that many HP
Describe Combat Cinematically

Don't just say "the goblin takes 6 damage." Say "your blade bites into the goblin's shoulder — it staggers, green blood running down its arm, and snarls at you." Good description makes even a failed attack feel dramatic.

Keeping Combat Moving

  • If a player takes more than 30 seconds on their turn — ask "what does [character name] do?" to prompt action
  • Move enemy turns quickly — enemies aren't the stars
  • Use a visible initiative tracker (sticky notes, index cards, a whiteboard)
  • When enemies reach half HP — have them react: flee, call for help, change tactics
  • Let the last enemy surrender if it makes for good roleplay
05

Roleplay & NPCs

🎭

Creating Memorable NPCs

Players remember NPCs who feel real — not because of elaborate backstories, but because of distinctive traits that show up in every interaction.

Physical Quirks

  • Taps fingers when nervous
  • Never makes eye contact
  • Has a distinctive laugh
  • Constantly cleaning/organizing
  • Speaks very quietly

Speech Patterns

  • Uses sailor slang
  • Refers to themselves in third person
  • Ends every statement as a question?
  • Extremely formal and verbose
  • Short, clipped answers. Doesn't elaborate.

Voicing NPCs — Accent Tips

You don't need to be a voice actor. You need contrast between NPCs so players can tell them apart.

  • Pitch: One NPC speaks high, one speaks low — instantly distinguishable
  • Speed: Fast-talker vs. slow, deliberate speaker
  • Volume: Whisperer vs. someone who always seems to be projecting
  • Vocabulary: Simple words vs. flowery language vs. jargon
The 3-Voice Rule

Prepare 3 distinct voices before your session: one raspy/low, one high-pitched/nervous, one warm/friendly. Assign them to your 3 main NPCs. You can improvise variations on the fly for others.

Improv Techniques for DMs

The core principle of improv is "Yes, and..." — accept what players offer and build on it. Translated to D&D:

  • "I want to seduce the dragon" → Don't say no. Say "roll Persuasion — the dragon raises an eyebrow and says..."
  • "Is the innkeeper secretly a spy?" → Now she is. Work backwards to establish why.
  • "I dive through the window" → Roll Athletics. On success: cinematic escape. On failure: hilarious window frame situation.

NPC Motivation = Believability

Every NPC should want something. Even the baker wants to sell bread and close early. When NPCs pursue their own goals even when players aren't around, the world feels alive.

Quick NPC Generator
Roll d6 for Goal1-Money 2-Safety 3-Revenge 4-Love 5-Power 6-Knowledge
Roll d6 for Obstacle1-Poverty 2-Enemy 3-Illness 4-Secrecy 5-Duty 6-Fear
Roll d6 for Trait1-Greedy 2-Brave 3-Cowardly 4-Kind 5-Bitter 6-Curious

Describing the World

Use all five senses — most DMs describe only visuals. Add one non-visual detail to every scene:

  • "The cave smells of damp stone and something rotting."
  • "The market is deafening — a hundred voices haggling at once."
  • "The dungeon floor is cold through your boots."
  • "You taste iron on your tongue — blood from where you bit your cheek in the fall."

The Golden Rules

📖

The 10 Commandments of the First-Time DM

#RuleWhy It Matters
1Never prepare a "correct" pathPlayers will immediately take a different one. Prepare situations, not scripts.
2Let dice fall where they fallFudging rolls to protect players removes stakes. Natural 1s and 20s create the best stories.
3Be consistent with your worldIf fire burns the villain, it burns the hero. Rules should apply equally.
4Say "yes, and" more than "no"Blocking player ideas kills momentum. Build on them instead.
5Don't narrate player emotionsNever say "you feel afraid." Say "the creature looks terrifying — how does [name] react?"
6Give every player a momentIf one player is dominating, find a scenario that lets quieter players shine.
7Consequences must feel fairPlayers accept failure if it followed logically. They riot if it feels arbitrary.
8Check in with your playersA quick "how are we doing?" break keeps everyone comfortable and engaged.
9Rules disputes go to break timeNever stop the game to look something up. Make a call, write it down, settle it after.
10Have fun yourselfDM energy is contagious. If you're excited, they're excited. Your enthusiasm is the session.

When to Say Yes vs. No

✅ Say Yes When...

  • It's creative and fun
  • It doesn't undermine others' fun
  • Failure is still interesting
  • It moves the story forward
  • You just think it would be cool

🚫 Say No When...

  • It would hurt another player (not their character)
  • It would end the session immediately with no fun
  • It violates the session's agreed tone
  • It requires you to lie about the world's rules

Player Engagement Tips

  • Use character names constantly — "What does Theron do?" feels more personal than "what do you do?"
  • Tie encounters to backstories — If a player has a dead sister, have an NPC mention a girl matching her description in the next town
  • Spotlight introverted players — Directly address them: "Mirella, you notice something the others missed — roll Perception"
  • Celebrate good ideas — "That's brilliant — roll for it" validates player creativity
  • Lose gracefully — When players outsmart your plan, be visibly delighted. It teaches them the game rewards cleverness.
Common Beginner Mistakes
  • DMPC (DM's own player character) — Don't give yourself a character that joins the party. You already play everyone else.
  • Monologuing villains — Keep villain speeches to 30 seconds max. Then something happens.
  • Railroading — If players feel their choices don't matter, they disengage. Always honor the illusion of choice, at minimum.
  • Rewarding passivity — Make sure cautious, quiet players still feel engaged. Ask them questions directly.

Session Zero — Do This Before Session One

A 30-minute "Session Zero" before your first real session saves enormous headaches:

  • Discuss what kind of game everyone wants (dark, comedic, heroic?)
  • Establish safety tools — is there any content that's off-limits?
  • Confirm player character concepts don't conflict or undermine each other
  • Set expectations: how long per session? How often do you meet?

Tools & Resources

🧰

Dice You Need

d4 d6 d8 d10 d12 d20 d100

You need at least one full set. Ideally two d6s (for rolling 2d6 damage, etc.) and two d20s (for Advantage/Disadvantage — roll both, take higher/lower).

Advantage & Disadvantage

Advantage: Roll 2d20, take the higher result. Granted when a player has a meaningful edge.

Disadvantage: Roll 2d20, take the lower result. Applied when something hinders the roll.

Session Checklist

✅

Print this page. Use it every session. The checkboxes are functional — check them off as you go.

Before the Session (Day Before)
  • Hook defined — I know how the session opens
  • 2–3 locations prepped with sensory details and NPCs
  • At least 1 combat encounter ready (enemy stats noted)
  • 2–3 NPCs prepped (name + trait + goal)
  • Possible endings considered (success, failure, partial)
  • Music/ambiance playlist ready
  • Printed or written notes in easy-to-reference format
Setup (30 Min Before Players Arrive)
  • Table cleared, snacks/drinks ready
  • Dice accessible and within reach
  • DM screen up (if using), notes behind it
  • Battle mat/grid ready if using miniatures
  • Music playing when players arrive (sets the mood immediately)
  • Phone on silent / Do Not Disturb
  • Initiative tracker ready (index cards or whiteboard)
Opening (First 15 Minutes)
  • Brief recap of last session (or intro if first session)
  • Opening scene delivered — players oriented to location + situation
  • "What do you do?" — spotlight on players
  • First 10 minutes: something interesting has already happened
During Play
  • Every player has spoken/acted at least once in the last 20 minutes
  • Pacing feels right — not too rushed, not too slow
  • No rules dispute has stalled the game more than 60 seconds
  • Notes taken on any player decisions that should have future consequences
Closing (Final 20 Minutes)
  • Combat/main challenge resolved
  • Loot awarded (or promised)
  • XP awarded (or milestone leveling noted)
  • Hook for next session teased or established
  • Quick debrief: what did players love? What was confusing?
  • Notes made for next session prep
End Every Session With This Question

"What was your favorite moment tonight?" — It takes 2 minutes, tells you what's working, and sends everyone home on a positive note.

The 6 Ability Scores — What They're Used For

STR — Strength

Lifting, pushing, breaking, melee attacks (usually). Athletics skill.

DEX — Dexterity

Dodging, sneaking, ranged attacks, AC. Acrobatics, Stealth, Sleight of Hand.

CON — Constitution

HP, concentration, holding breath, endurance. No skills directly tied to it.

INT — Intelligence

Knowledge, reasoning, magic (Wizards). Arcana, History, Investigation, Nature, Religion.

WIS — Wisdom

Perception, insight, awareness. Animal Handling, Insight, Medicine, Perception, Survival.

CHA — Charisma

Social influence, force of personality. Deception, Intimidation, Performance, Persuasion.

Common DM Rulings — When You're Not Sure

  • Can they do this? → Pick an ability score + skill, set a DC, let them roll
  • Is this possible? → If it's physically/logically possible in the fiction, yes. Assign a DC.
  • Does this kill them? → Massive damage rule: if damage equals max HP or double remaining HP, they must make a DC 15 Constitution save or instantly die
  • Do they know this information? → Relevant skill (Arcana, History, Nature, Religion) vs. DC 10–20 based on obscurity
  • Does the trap trigger? → Passive Perception (10 + Perception modifier) to notice, or DC to spot if actively searching

Inspiration

Award Inspiration when a player does something that embodies their character beautifully — great roleplay, honoring their flaw, acting in-character even when it costs them. A player with Inspiration can spend it for Advantage on any roll. Only one Inspiration can be held at a time.

Short vs. Long Rest

Short Rest (1 hour)

Players can spend Hit Dice to heal. Some class features recharge (Warlock spells, Fighter Second Wind).

Long Rest (8 hours)

Full HP restored. All Hit Dice restored (half max). Most spells slots and features recharge.

Your Most Important Tool

The phrase "That's a great idea — roll for it." Use it whenever a player proposes something interesting, unexpected, or creative. It validates their agency, creates tension with the die roll, and lets the fiction emerge organically from the result. Master this phrase and you've mastered 80% of DMing.

1. Role Overview: Director, Americas Sales Leader – Operational Technology

What This Role Involves

This is a senior sales leadership position responsible for driving ServiceNow's Operational Technology (OT) business across the Americas region. You won't be carrying a bag—you'll be building the engine.

Core Responsibilities:

  • Team Leadership: Lead and develop a team of OT Solution Sales Executives across multiple territories
  • Strategic Planning: Architect territory strategies to maximize vertical alignment (manufacturing, energy, utilities, transportation)
  • Use Case Identification: Guide teams to identify OT-specific use cases that resonate with industrial operations leaders
  • Cross-Functional Orchestration: Collaborate with presales (like Luke), product, marketing, and alliances to ensure cohesive go-to-market execution
  • Executive Engagement: Build relationships with C-suite and operational leaders at target accounts

The OT Market Context

Operational Technology (OT) refers to hardware and software that monitors and controls physical industrial processes—think manufacturing plant floor systems, energy grid management, water treatment facilities, and transportation logistics.

Why OT Matters Now: Digital transformation has exploded the number of connected devices in operational environments. OT/IT convergence creates massive security and management challenges—ServiceNow is positioning as the platform that unifies visibility, security, and operations across both domains.

Key Market Drivers:

  • Cybersecurity Imperatives: OT breaches can halt production, disrupt critical infrastructure, or endanger public safety
  • Asset Visibility Gaps: Many organizations lack comprehensive inventory of their OT devices (PLCs, HMIs, sensors, SCADA systems)
  • Compliance & Regulation: Increasing regulatory pressure on critical infrastructure sectors
  • Operational Efficiency: Predictive maintenance, uptime optimization, automated workflows

Leadership Expectations

At the Director level, ServiceNow expects:

  • Player-Coach Mentality: You're building a team while still engaging in strategic deals
  • Vertical Expertise: Deep understanding of industrial operations and how technology transformation happens in these sectors
  • Solution Selling Mastery: Complex, consultative sales cycles with 6-18 month deal timelines
  • Executive Presence: Ability to engage CIOs, COOs, and operational VPs

What Success Looks Like

  • Pipeline growth in target OT verticals
  • Team quota attainment and skill development
  • Strategic account penetration (landing + expanding)
  • Thought leadership positioning in OT market

4. Likely Interview Questions & Talking Points

Question 1: "How would you approach building a sales strategy for OT in a new vertical, say energy & utilities?"

Framework to Use: Demonstrate structured thinking—market research → ICP definition → account selection → stakeholder mapping → value proposition customization.

Willie's Suggested Response:

"I'd start with deep vertical immersion—understanding the operational priorities and pain points specific to energy & utilities. In this sector, you're dealing with grid reliability, regulatory compliance (NERC CIP for electric utilities), aging infrastructure, and increasing renewable integration complexity."

"From there, I'd define our Ideal Customer Profile: utilities with significant OT footprints, existing ServiceNow IT deployments (land-and-expand opportunity), recent security incidents or audit findings, and digital transformation initiatives underway."

"For stakeholder mapping, energy companies typically have siloed decision-making—the CIO owns IT platforms, the VP of Operations or Chief Engineer owns OT systems, and the CISO is increasingly involved due to cyber risks. Our sales motion needs to engage all three, with Operations as the operational buyer and IT/Security as the technical/economic buyers."

"Finally, I'd work closely with presales and product marketing to tailor our value proposition: for utilities, that's about resilience, compliance, and threat visibility rather than just efficiency. We're helping them meet regulatory mandates while protecting critical infrastructure."

Question 2: "Tell me about a time you led a team through a significant market shift or transformation."

Willie's Example: Draw from your experience at Megaport/Latitude.sh during the cloud connectivity evolution. Frame it as:
  • Situation: Market shifting from MPLS to cloud-first networking
  • Challenge: Sales team trained on traditional telecom sale, not cloud infrastructure
  • Action: Built enablement program, partnered with technical teams, redefined ICP, coached reps through first cloud-native deals
  • Result: Team quota attainment improved, pipeline quality increased, reps became fluent in cloud architecture conversations

Bridge to OT: "The parallel I see with OT is similar—you're asking industrial operations teams to think differently about visibility, security, and orchestration. It requires sales reps who can bridge the technical depth of OT environments with the business outcomes executives care about."

Question 3: "How do you qualify and prioritize accounts when selling complex infrastructure solutions?"

Framework to Use: Reference a qualification methodology (MEDDPICC, BANT+, etc.) but customize for OT context.

Willie's Suggested Response:

"I use an adapted MEDDPICC framework, with some OT-specific nuances:"

  • Metrics: What's the business impact? In OT, that might be unplanned downtime costs, regulatory fines avoided, or risk reduction quantified.
  • Economic Buyer: Who controls budget? In OT deals, this is often split—IT budget for platform, Operations budget for deployment services.
  • Decision Criteria: What are they evaluating? OT buyers care deeply about non-disruptive deployment—you can't take a production line offline to install software.
  • Decision Process: OT deals often require operational trials, security reviews, and cross-functional steering committees. Understanding this upfront prevents surprises.
  • Pain: Is there a compelling event? A recent breach, upcoming audit, M&A integration, or new regulatory deadline?
  • Champion: Who's selling internally on our behalf? In OT, your champion is often a forward-thinking Operations leader or CISO.

"I prioritize accounts with clear pain, identified champions, and realistic timelines. In infrastructure sales, patience and rigor in qualification prevents wasted cycles on deals that won't close."

Question 4: "How do you work with presales teams to drive complex deals?"

Luke's Testing: This is personal for him—he wants to know you'll be a great partner to his team.

Willie's Suggested Response:

"Presales is a strategic partner, not a resource to be deployed. The best sales outcomes happen when account executives and SEs co-own the deal strategy from qualification through close."

"My approach: involve presales early in discovery. Before we ever do a demo, I want the SE to hear the customer's pain firsthand, understand their environment, and co-develop the solution narrative with me. That way, when we present, it's not a generic pitch—it's a tailored solution that resonates."

"I also protect SE time ruthlessly. I won't pull a presales engineer into a deal unless it's qualified and we have a clear outcome for that engagement. That mutual respect builds trust and ensures they're excited to go deep on the deals that matter."

"In previous roles, I've run joint win/loss reviews with presales teams to continuously improve our approach. The feedback loop makes both sides better."

Question 5: "What's your understanding of the OT landscape? How does it differ from IT sales?"

Show You've Done Your Homework:

Willie's Suggested Response:

"OT is fundamentally different from IT in a few key ways:"

  1. Uptime is sacred. In IT, you can patch a server and reboot. In OT, a 30-second disruption can halt a production line or disrupt a power grid. Any solution we propose has to be non-intrusive and operationally tested.
  2. Long asset lifecycles. IT refreshes every 3-5 years. OT devices (PLCs, SCADA systems) can run for 15-20 years. You're often dealing with legacy systems that were never designed to be networked or monitored remotely.
  3. Different stakeholders. IT sales engage CIOs and IT directors. OT sales require relationships with plant managers, operations VPs, safety officers, and engineering teams—people who think in terms of throughput, yield, and safety incidents, not SaaS metrics.
  4. Security is existential. An IT breach leaks data. An OT breach can cause physical harm, environmental damage, or disrupt critical services. The risk calculus is different, and buyers need to be convinced that your solution won't introduce new vulnerabilities.

"That said, there's a convergence happening. OT teams are starting to adopt IT best practices—asset management, change control, workflow automation. ServiceNow is uniquely positioned to help them make that transition without sacrificing operational integrity."

Question 6: "Why ServiceNow? Why OT?"

Willie's Suggested Response:

"Two reasons: platform and timing."

"First, ServiceNow is the only platform that can truly unify IT and OT operations. Every other player is either an OT-native vendor trying to bolt on IT capabilities, or an ITSM platform treating OT as an afterthought. ServiceNow has the enterprise credibility, the workflow engine, and now with Armis, the OT security depth to be the system of record for both domains."

"Second, the timing is perfect. The market is at an inflection point—regulations are tightening, breaches are increasing, and executives finally understand that OT risk is enterprise risk. This isn't a nice-to-have anymore; it's a boardroom conversation. Being part of that transformation, helping critical infrastructure companies secure and optimize their operations, is exactly the kind of high-impact work I want to be doing."

"And candidly, I'm drawn to the challenge. OT is a complex sale that requires technical credibility, strategic thinking, and the ability to navigate organizational complexity. That's where I thrive."

Question 7: "Where do you see the OT market headed in the next 3-5 years?"

Willie's Suggested Response:

"I see three major trends:"

  1. Convergence accelerates. The wall between IT and OT is coming down. You'll see more CISOs with OT responsibility, more joint budgets, and more demand for unified platforms that manage both.
  2. AI-driven operations. Predictive maintenance and anomaly detection in OT environments will become table stakes. Companies that can surface insights from OT data and automate responses will win.
  3. Regulatory pressure intensifies. Governments are waking up to the risks of insecure OT. We'll see more mandates, more audits, and more urgency around compliance. That creates a tailwind for platforms like ServiceNow that can help companies meet those requirements.

"The companies that figure this out will have a massive competitive advantage. The ones that don't will face operational disruptions, security incidents, and falling behind. That's the opportunity for ServiceNow—and for this role."
